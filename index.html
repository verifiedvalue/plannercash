How planner.cash works

planner.cash is two planners that share one set of numbers: a long-range debt vs. invest simulator and a paycheck-level cash calendar. Nothing is double-entered—you set loans, checking, income, and bills on the Dashboard; budget/strategy controls on Debt payoff use that same state.

Where each tab fits

Dashboard is the control room: loan balances/minimums and investments on one side, auto-filled monthly obligations on the other, plus checking, emergency fund targets, income, bills, manual savings, and manual investment transfers. The overview at the top highlights cash flow and debt, with investments, savings, and a single health score; savings pace follows your paycheck plan (optimized when you have run analysis).

Debt payoff is for charts and controls—extra cash, return, horizon, strategy comparison, net worth/debt trajectories, per-loan detail, and month-by-month breakdown.

Paycheck planner is for timing: calendar, paycheck-by-paycheck set-asides, budget overview, and insights. Your manual plan always previews 12 months from the Dashboard inputs (including your checking cash floor). Apply optimized plan (1 year) (Premium) runs the lookahead optimizer without a separate dialog; Optimize in the header is reserved for a future subscriber workflow and does not change behavior yet.

The three strategies

Avalanche Highest interest rate first

Pay the minimum on every loan. Put all extra cash toward the loan with the highest interest rate. Once it's paid off, redirect to the next highest rate. This is mathematically optimal — it minimizes total interest paid.

Saves the most money in interest
Fastest net worth growth
May take longer to see first payoff
Requires discipline without early wins

Snowball Smallest balance first

Pay the minimum on every loan. Put all extra cash toward the loan with the smallest balance. Paying off loans quickly creates psychological momentum — proven to help people stay on track.

Quick early wins feel motivating
Simplifies your number of loans fast
Costs more in total interest
Slower net worth growth mathematically

Minimum only Invest first

Pay only the minimums on all loans. Invest all extra cash immediately. Bets that investment returns will outpace interest costs. Works best when loan rates are significantly below market returns.

Portfolio compounds from day one
Wins when rate spread favors markets
Pays maximum total interest
Debt lingers longer; higher monthly stress

The math behind net worth

Net worth = portfolio value minus remaining debt. Each month, every dollar is allocated: first to loan minimums, then to the target loan (per strategy) or investments. Once a loan hits zero, its freed-up minimum payment is redirected. The minimum-only strategy shows what happens when extra cash compounds in the market while debt decays slowly—whether that wins depends on rates, balances, and horizon.

Why the payoff order matters on multi-loan debt

With a single loan, avalanche and snowball are the same. With multiple loans, the order of payoff changes how quickly freed-up minimums compound into your extra cash — which changes how aggressively you can attack remaining debt. A $50/mo minimum on a paid-off loan becomes extra firepower for the next one. planner.cash models this cascade precisely.
